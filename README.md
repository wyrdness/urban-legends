## Overview

Urban legends — preferred in academic folklore studies as 'contemporary legends' — are a recognized genre of folklore consisting of stories told as true (or potentially true) accounts that circulate orally, in print, and digitally, are usually unverifiable, and turn on themes of modern anxiety: cars, highways, hospitals, technology, food, sex, crime, and the body. The American folklorist Richard M. Dorson is generally credited with introducing 'urban legend' to academic discourse around 1968, but the term entered general English-language usage through the work of Jan Harold Brunvand, professor of English at the University of Utah, beginning with The Vanishing Hitchhiker: American Urban Legends and Their Meanings (1981). Urban legends share recognizable structural features: a 'friend-of-a-friend' (FOAF) attribution, a memorable plot turn, often a moral or cautionary edge, and continual local adaptation. Foundational older studies include Beardsley and Hankey's analysis of the Vanishing Hitchhiker (California Folklore Quarterly, 1942–1943) and Linda Dégh's work on legend-telling. The Internet era has transformed urban-legend transmission: chain emails, FOAF-attributed message-board posts, and 'creepypasta' (e.g. Slender Man, born from a 2009 Something Awful forum thread by Eric Knudsen) demonstrate that the genre's mechanisms — anonymous attribution, plausibility, communal authorship — are if anything more vigorous online than in oral culture. Snopes (founded 1994 by David and Barbara Mikkelson) became the standard public-facing debunking resource for urban legends and remains one of the most cited fact-checking websites today.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: American English coinage, mid-20th century. Folklorist Richard M. Dorson is widely credited with introducing 'urban legend' as an academic term around 1968; the term went mainstream through Jan Harold Brunvand's books from 1981 onward.
- **Meaning**: 'Legend' (a narrative told as true) of 'urban' (modern, contemporary, mass-society) life — distinguishing such tales from older rural or 'primitive' folklore.
- **First use**: Academic use c. 1968 (Dorson); popular use from 1981 (Brunvand)

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Folkloric processing of social anxiety** _(Jan Harold Brunvand, Bill Ellis, Linda Dégh, Gary Alan Fine)_

Urban legends function as a collective vehicle for processing modern anxieties — about strangers, technology, food, sex, crime, and corporate power — reinforcing community norms while exploring 'safe' fears.

**Memetic / cognitive selection** _(Pascal Boyer, Chip and Dan Heath (Made to Stick, 2007))_

Urban legends survive because their structure — minimal counter-intuitive elements, vivid imagery, emotional charge — is memorable and easily retransmitted.

**Networked / participatory legend** _(Trevor J. Blank, Lynne S. McNeill, Andrew Peck)_

Online urban legends emerge through participatory authorship across forums and social media; their structure differs from oral legends in that authorship is collective and traceable.

### Skeptical

**Empirical falsifiability** _(Snopes (Mikkelson), Joe Nickell)_

Most named urban legends, when investigated, can be shown to be false, vastly distorted, or untraceable to a documented original event.
